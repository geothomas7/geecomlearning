## Posting rules (read first)

1. **Never put the article URL in the post body.** LinkedIn's algorithm penalises external links in the body. Drop the link in the **first comment**, immediately after the post goes live.
2. **Post from the Geecom Learning Company Page, not a personal profile**, then have team members reshare to extend reach.
3. **One post per week** for the series — six posts total, three per article. Posting cadence: Tuesdays or Wednesdays at 9:00 AM PT for best B2B engagement.
4. **Hashtags** go at the bottom of the post body. Mix one large industry tag, two mid-size, and one Canadian-specific tag.
5. **No emojis. No "🚀" or "💡" openers. No "Hot take:" or "Unpopular opinion:" framings.**

---

## Why these posts are written this way (SEO + GEO design notes)

These posts are designed to do two jobs at once: drive clicks to the resource articles (SEO funnel) **and** be quotable enough to be picked up by AI engines (Generative Engine Optimization). LinkedIn content is increasingly crawled by ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews. A post that gets cited in an AI answer reaches an audience the LinkedIn feed never would.

Every post in this file follows the same five rules:

1. **Lead with a specific number from the report.** ("Only 15% of employees…", "88% of organisations…", "36% of organisations qualify…"). Generic openers do not get cited.
2. **Name the source publication in the post body.** "LinkedIn's 2025 Workplace Learning Report" appears in every post — never just "the report" or "a recent study". AI engines match by named entity.
3. **Include at least one self-contained, quotable sentence.** A sentence that makes sense if screenshot on its own. AI engines pull individual sentences out of context — write each post so it has at least one extractable line.
4. **Name the geographic and audience context.** "Canadian SMBs" or "small and mid-sized employers" appears in every post. This is what makes the post relevant to our specific audience and distinct from the millions of generic L&D posts in the corpus.
5. **Closing line points to the article without the URL.** The CTA in body uses phrases like "We unpacked this in our latest piece" or "Link in the comments" — the URL itself goes in the first comment.

These rules apply to **every future LinkedIn post** for any resource article, not just this series. They are codified in `resource-article-guidelines.md` Part 11.

## Post 1.1 — The skills concentration problem

Half of L&D professionals say their executives are worried that employees do not have the right skills to execute the business strategy.

LinkedIn's 2025 Workplace Learning Report calls it a skills crisis.

What the report does not say plainly: the skills most at risk of being lost to attrition are exactly the ones that hold a small business together. Business strategy. Strategic planning. Sales management. Operations management. Team leadership.

Number one through five on LinkedIn's at-risk list.

At a 1,000-person company, when one of these people leaves, someone else absorbs the gap. At a 60-person company, the gap is the company.

The starting point for any SMB looking at this report is not a training plan. It is a one-page list of which strategic skills are concentrated in single individuals — and what happens if those people walk on Friday.

## Post 1.2 — The retention finding people miss

88% of organisations told LinkedIn they are concerned about employee retention.

When asked which retention strategy they were betting on, the number one answer was learning opportunities.

Not compensation. Not flexibility. Not benefits.

Learning came first.

This finding has been showing up in research for years and most SMBs still treat training as a cost line rather than a retention investment. The companies that get this right do not necessarily spend more — they make their training visible, tied to real work, and connected to what someone could become rather than just what they need to know to do today's job.

The hardest version of the retention conversation is also the most honest one: if a strong employee asked you tomorrow, "what could I learn here that I could not learn anywhere else?" — what would you actually say?

## Post 1.3 — The manager support collapse

The most under-reported finding in the 2025 LinkedIn Workplace Learning Report:

Only 15% of employees say their manager helped them build a career development plan in the last six months.

That number is down five percentage points from 2024. Every measure of manager-led career support dropped year over year — encouragement to learn, recommendations of learning materials, challenges to build new skills.

This is not a problem of willingness. Half of survey respondents said managers lack the support they need to do this work. Managers are out of capacity.

For enterprises with structured talent functions, other systems can partially fill the gap. For a Canadian SMB, the manager is the entire career development infrastructure. If managers are not having career conversations, no one is.

The fix is not a leadership development programme. It is a single repeatable conversation template that takes 20 minutes and gets used every quarter. The barrier is rarely sophistication. It is the absence of a default tool to reach for.

## Post 2.2 — Internal mobility, scaled down

Career development champions in LinkedIn's 2025 report are nearly twice as likely to have a formal internal mobility programme.

Most of the case studies describe AI-powered talent marketplaces and global pipeline programmes.

In a 75-person company, internal mobility is not a marketplace. It is a list.

A quarterly leadership team conversation with one question: who is ready for more responsibility, and what would "more" look like for them?

The answers go on a spreadsheet. The spreadsheet gets revisited every 90 days. When a role opens, that document gets read before any external posting goes out.

Most internal moves do not die because no one is qualified. They die because no one started the conversation in time.

Three quiet practices separate companies that promote from within from companies that say they want to: a list, a quarterly review, and a five-minute pre-posting conversation between the hiring manager and HR.

## Post 2.3 — Measuring without an LMS

LinkedIn's 2025 Workplace Learning Report is full of impressive measurement infrastructure. Engagement scores. Promotion velocity. Skills assessment platforms. Learning analytics dashboards.

Most Canadian SMBs do not have any of this. Many never will.

Three numbers tracked manually every quarter are enough for a company under 500 employees:

1. Percentage of employees who had a career conversation with their manager in the last 90 days. Target: 80% or higher.

2. Number of internal moves and promotions in the last 90 days, plus the count of internal candidates seriously considered for any open role.

3. Number of employees who completed at least one piece of skill-building learning — a course, a certification, or a documented stretch assignment.

A shared spreadsheet. A quarterly people review meeting. That is the entire system.

The point of measurement is not the precision. It is the conversation it forces. When leaders see that no career conversations happened in Operations last quarter, the next quarter looks different.
