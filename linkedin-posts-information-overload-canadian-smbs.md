## Post 1 — The 38% number

38% of managers report feeling overwhelmed by workplace communication.

That figure comes from Gartner's January 2025 research on information overload. The same study found that more than a quarter of all employees feel the same way — and that the cost shows up as lower strategic alignment, reduced intent to stay, and millions of dollars in lost productivity.

For a Canadian SMB, the number is almost certainly worse than the headline implies. Why? Because Gartner's framing assumes there is an internal communications team filtering and sequencing messages. At most small and mid-sized Canadian employers, there is no such filter. Every operational lead has direct access to the all-staff channel, and uses it freely.

The fix is rarely a new tool. It is a one-page channel rulebook and a single named approver for all-staff messages.

## Post 2 — Gartner's four-characteristic test

Gartner's 2025 research on information overload offers a diagnostic tool that takes about twenty minutes to run and almost always changes how a leadership team thinks about internal communication.

The framework: a set of workplace information becomes burdensome when it is one of four things —

— Duplicative: similar information arrives in a compressed timeframe across multiple channels.
— Effort-intensive: hard to find or reference when an employee actually needs it.
— Inconsistent: different sources tell employees different things about the same topic.
— Irrelevant: messages reach people whose work is not affected.

Most Canadian SMBs we work with score themselves at three out of four on any given week. The exception is usually inconsistency, which is harder to spot from the inside.

The exercise itself is simple. Print the list, walk through last week's all-staff messages, tag each one against the four characteristics. The pattern that emerges is the case for change — and it is more persuasive than any framework you could borrow from a McKinsey deck.

## Post 3 — The L&D angle no one talks about

Information overload is usually framed as a communications problem.

For Canadian SMBs without an internal comms team, it is also a learning problem in disguise.

Look at the all-staff messages your business sends in a typical month. A meaningful share of them are policy reminders, procedural clarifications, and how-tos that someone has explained five times before. Each one of those messages is the absence of a stable internal reference document.

Gartner's January 2025 information overload research recommends "enhancing information relevance" through audits and tailoring. For a 60-person company without a comms team, the simpler version is this: convert recurring reminder messages into short, on-demand learning assets. A one-page guide. A two-minute video. An item in the onboarding library. Every reminder you can replace with a stable reference is one fewer all-staff message, one less load on managers, and one less thing to chase.

This is the cheapest part of the information overload fix and the part most Canadian small and mid-sized employers skip — because it does not feel like a communications project. It is a learning project.
