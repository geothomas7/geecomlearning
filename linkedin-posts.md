## Post 1 — Psychological Safety at Work

**Hook (93 chars):**
Most leaders think psychological safety means being nice. It means something harder than that.

---

A manager I know ran a tight ship. Meetings on time. Decisions fast. Every deadline met.

He also had a turnover problem he couldn't explain.

Exit interview after exit interview said the same thing: people didn't feel safe raising concerns. Not because he was unkind — because he was certain. Raising a different view felt pointless.

Psychological safety isn't about being gentle. It's about creating conditions where people can disagree, admit mistakes, and ask questions without expecting consequences.

Teams that lack it don't speak up until problems become unavoidable.

Harvard research showed psychologically safe teams caught and corrected errors faster than teams that avoided conflict.

This is not a soft skill. It is a performance driver.

What does your workplace do well — or poorly — around psychological safety?

## Post 2 — Mental Health at Worksites

**Hook (102 chars):**
A construction site has a safety plan for every physical hazard. Almost none have one for mental health.

---

A supervisor told me: "We have toolbox talks for slips and falls. We have nothing for the guy drowning in debt working 14-hour shifts."

Construction workers have one of the highest suicide rates of any trade. The work is demanding, schedules are gruelling, and the culture around asking for help has been — to put it plainly — hostile.

But this is changing. And employers are a significant lever.

Not because an EAP poster fixes anything. Because supervisors trained to have a real conversation. Because shift structures that don't systematically destroy sleep. Because a culture where saying "I'm struggling" doesn't cost someone their credibility.

These aren't soft interventions. They are operational decisions.

The employers we work with in trades are starting to treat mental health the same way they treat physical safety: as a risk management issue.

Because it is.

What does your workplace do when someone on site is visibly not okay?

#MentalHealthAtWork #WorkplaceSafety #Construction #HRManagers #EmployerObligations

## Post 3 — Right to Say No

**Hook (97 chars):**
Your team has a right to refuse unsafe work. Most of them don't know they can actually use it.

---

Most workers in Canada have a legal right to refuse unsafe work.

Most have no idea how to use it, what counts as "unsafe," or what happens next if they do.

I've spoken with HR managers who discovered this gap the hard way — an incident happened, someone asked why no one spoke up, and the honest answer was: people didn't think they could.

The right to refuse is in OHS legislation across every province. But legislation doesn't change behaviour. Training does.

There's a real difference between a workforce that knows about this right in theory and one that understands:

— What conditions qualify
— What the process looks like
— That they won't be penalised for using it

The employers who take this seriously don't frame it as a liability. They frame it as: this is how we find problems before someone gets hurt.

Does your team know when they can stop work — and what to do next?

#WorkplaceSafety #EmployeeRights #OHS #HR #ComplianceTraining

## Post 4 — Compliance Training Value

**Hook (87 chars):**
Compliance training has a reputation for being terrible. That reputation is mostly earned.

---

Mandatory. Annual. Click through the slides. Pass the quiz.

No one learns anything meaningful. The box gets ticked. The record gets filed.

Then the incident happens that the training was supposed to prevent.

The problem isn't compliance training as a concept. It's that most compliance training is designed to meet a documentation requirement — not to change behaviour.

Those are two completely different briefs.

Training designed to generate a certificate will do exactly that.

Training designed to change what someone does in a real situation requires understanding the scenario, the person in it, and the decision they'll face.

One gives you audit documentation. The other gives you fewer incidents and a team that actually knows what to do when it matters.

The organisation that thinks compliance training is a waste of money has almost certainly never had compliance training that was built to work.

What would make compliance training worth your team's time?

## Post 5 — The Cost of Bad Onboarding

**Hook (98 chars):**
A new hire who leaves in the first 90 days costs around 50% of their annual salary to replace.

---

That's $15,000–$40,000 for most professional roles in Canada, once you account for recruiting, lost productivity, and management time.

Most organisations treat onboarding as an admin process. Fill out the forms. Meet the team. Find the coffee machine.

What it actually is: the period in which a new person decides whether they made the right choice.

The research is consistent. Companies with strong structured onboarding improve new hire retention by 82%, according to Brandon Hall Group research.

"Structured" means a clear first week, role-specific context delivered before the person is expected to perform, and someone accountable for making sure the information lands.

Not a stack of PDFs. Not a week of shadow shifts with no debrief.

The organisations that fixed their onboarding tell us the same thing afterward: they didn't realise how much the old approach was costing them until they saw what a good one produced.

What was the best onboarding you've experienced — as a new hire or as someone who built the programme?

## Post 6 — Retention ROI

**Hook (88 chars):**
The most expensive training decision most companies make is the one they keep delaying.

---

An organisation has a turnover problem. People leave at the 12–18 month mark. Exit interviews say vague things about "growth opportunities."

The response: more 1:1s. Better benefits. A new engagement survey.

What it rarely is: better training.

Not because training isn't the answer. Because training feels like a long-term investment while turnover feels like a problem that needs a fast fix.

But the data doesn't support that framing. Organisations with the lowest turnover in their sectors tend to share two things: good onboarding and a clear path for development after it.

Both are learning problems. Both have training solutions.

The LinkedIn Learning report found 94% of employees would stay longer at a company that invested in their career development.

If you're running a retention problem through HR levers and not moving the numbers — it might actually be a learning and development problem wearing a retention disguise.

Is turnover costing your organisation more than your training budget?

## Post 7 — What Microlearning Actually Is

**Hook (98 chars):**
Microlearning isn't short eLearning. It's a fundamentally different approach to how people retain.

---

There's a version of microlearning that is just a 7-minute eLearning module with a different name.

That's not what makes it work.

The principle is spaced repetition: people retain information better when it's delivered in small doses across time, not in one concentrated hit.

A one-hour compliance module delivered once a year is not how the brain stores anything reliably. Five 4-minute modules across two months, each building on the last — that's a different kind of retention.

Microlearning works best for:
— Policy updates that need to stick across a large team
— Safety knowledge that needs to stay current
— Skills that build sequentially

It doesn't replace deep skills training, complex onboarding, or anything where context matters as much as content.

The organisations that get the most from it use microlearning with intention — as a deliberate tool for retention, not a cheaper version of proper training.

What does your team currently use for reinforcement after initial training?

## Post 8 — Why Outsource L&D

**Hook (99 chars):**
Building an in-house L&D team sounds like the right move. For most SMBs, the numbers say otherwise.

---

The appeal of in-house is real. You want someone who knows your business, is always available, and doesn't need re-briefing every project.

The reality is messier.

A single in-house instructional designer costs $70,000–$95,000 per year in salary and benefits. They typically can't cover video production, LMS setup, and advanced eLearning without additional hires or tooling costs. And when they leave — which they will — you start over.

For most organisations under 500 people, this math doesn't work.

What does work: a clear, ongoing relationship with an external team that knows your training needs and carries the overhead of tooling, expertise, and production themselves.

The question isn't "should we do this in-house?" It's: "how many training modules do we actually need per year, and what does it cost to produce them?"

For organisations making 3–5 modules a year, outsourcing almost always wins when you count the full cost.

Have you done the full cost calculation of your current approach?
