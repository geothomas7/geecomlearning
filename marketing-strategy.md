# PART 1 — STRATEGY OVERVIEW

## Who We Are Talking To

HR managers, operations directors, and people leaders in British Columbia and across Canada who own the training problem without an internal L&D team.

## What We Are Trying to Achieve

1. Book discovery calls (pipeline)
2. Build brand awareness (reach)
3. Grow an email list (owned audience)
4. Position as a thought leader in workplace learning (trust)

These are not competing goals. The same content that educates a reader also builds trust, and trust converts a reader into a call booking.

---

## Brand Voice Principles

Every piece of content — LinkedIn post, email, blog — should sound like the same person wrote it.

- **Narrative-led:** Start with an observation, a story, or a moment before making the point.
- **Warm but evidence-backed:** You care about the reader's situation. You also know what works.
- **Plain language:** No jargon. No acronyms without explanation. No industry-insider language that loses a busy ops director at sentence two.
- **Employer-facing POV:** Write for the person who owns the training problem, not the learner.
- **Short punchy closing lines:** Earn the reader's attention, then land.
- **No emojis. No AI-template openers.** ("In today's fast-paced world..." is not how we open anything.)

# PART 2 — LINKEDIN

## Why LinkedIn First

Geecom's buyers are on LinkedIn. HR directors, L&D managers, operations leaders, and CEOs of growing SMBs in BC and across Canada use LinkedIn professionally. Organic LinkedIn content compounds — a post from month one still shows up in search months after.

## Posting Cadence

**Target: 3 posts per week**

- Monday: Insight or observation post (thought leadership)
- Wednesday: Story or case-based post (social proof, relatable problem)
- Friday: Practical tip or question post (engagement driver)

Once traction is established (connections 500+, regular comments), consider adding a fourth post mid-quarter.

## Content Pillars

**Pillar 1 — The Problem (30% of posts)**
Posts that name the training frustrations Geecom's buyers live with every day. Freelancer chaos. Compliance anxiety. Onboarding that takes four months. These posts generate the most shares and comments.

**Pillar 2 — The Evidence (25% of posts)**
Data-backed posts about the real cost of bad training, the ROI of getting it right, and what research says about how people actually learn. These establish credibility.

**Pillar 3 — The Approach (25% of posts)**
Posts that show how Geecom thinks about learning design. Microlearning vs. long-form. Why compliance training fails. What makes onboarding videos get watched. Thought leadership — not "here is our process," but "here is a better way to think about this."

**Pillar 4 — The Invitation (20% of posts)**
Softer posts that open a conversation. A question to the audience. A behind-the-scenes observation. These generate replies and direct conversations.

## Engagement Rules

- Respond to every comment within 24 hours. Early comments boost distribution.
- Comment on 5–10 posts per week from peers, clients, and potential buyers. Genuine observations, not "great post."
- Connect with every person who engages with your content. Send a short, non-pitchy message.
- Do not auto-post. Write each post freshly, or at minimum read it aloud before scheduling.

## Post Format Rules

- **Hook:** First line must stand alone and create a reason to click "see more." Under 110 characters.
- **Body:** 800–1,000 characters total. One idea per post. No walls of text — use line breaks liberally.
- **Close:** One CTA question that invites a comment.
- **Hashtags:** 3–5 at the end. Mix high-volume (#WorkplaceTraining, #HR) and niche (#eLearning, #ComplianceTraining, #L&D).
- **No links in the post itself.** LinkedIn suppresses posts with external links. Put the link in the first comment if you need it.

### Post 7 — What Microlearning Actually Is

**Hook:** Microlearning isn't short eLearning. It's a fundamentally different approach to how people retain.

---

There's a version of microlearning that is just a 7-minute eLearning module with a different name.

That's not what makes it work.

The principle is spaced repetition: people retain information better when it's delivered in small doses across time, not in one concentrated hit.

A one-hour compliance module delivered once a year is not how the brain stores anything reliably. Five 4-minute modules across two months, each building on the last — that's a different kind of retention.

Microlearning works best for:
— Policy updates that need to stick across a large team
— Safety knowledge that needs to stay current
— Skills that build sequentially

It doesn't replace deep skills training, complex onboarding, or anything where context matters as much as content.

The organisations that get the most from it use microlearning with intention — as a deliberate tool for retention, not a cheaper version of proper training.

## Why Build an Email List

A LinkedIn following is rented. An email list is owned. If LinkedIn changes its algorithm, your reach can drop overnight. An email subscriber has raised their hand — that is a fundamentally warmer audience.

## Opt-In Hook

**The Newsletter Promise:** "One short email, once a month. Practical L&D insight for the people who own the training problem without a full team." Add as a signup block to the footer of every page.

## Send Cadence

**Monthly.** One well-crafted email per month beats four rushed ones. Readers learn to expect it. The goal is reliability, not frequency.

## Content Structure (Each Issue)

1. **Opening line:** One observation or story from the month. 2–3 sentences.
2. **Main piece:** One useful idea in full. 300–400 words. Not a summary of a blog post — the actual substance.
3. **One link:** A single relevant resource. Track clicks, not opens.
4. **Closing CTA:** One clear action. Usually "book a call" or "reply to this email."

## Subject Line Rules

- Keep subject lines under 50 characters
- Avoid spam triggers: "free," "guaranteed," "click here," "limited time"
- Best subject lines are either specific ("Why compliance training fails at 92% completion") or intriguing ("The 35-second problem")
- A/B test when list exceeds 500 subscribers

### Issue 2 — Compliance

**Subject:** Why compliance training fails at 92% completion
**Preview text:** The documentation exists. The behaviour didn't change.

---

Hi [First Name],

There is a compliance training failure mode I see regularly: the completion rate is 92%, the records are clean, and then an incident happens that the training was supposed to prevent.

This is not a documentation problem. It is a design problem.

Compliance training built to generate a certificate is a different brief from compliance training built to change behaviour in a specific situation. Most organisations are commissioning the first one while hoping for the second.

The design difference is not subtle:

Training built for documentation: long module, passive narration, multiple-choice quiz at the end, certificate issued. Learner clicks through, retains almost nothing.

Training built for behaviour change: short scenarios that put the learner in the situation they will actually face, decisions that mirror real workplace choices, immediate feedback on what they got wrong and why. Learner walks away with a mental model they can use.

The second approach is not significantly more expensive to produce. It takes more thought to design. The instructional designer has to understand the job, not just the regulation.

When we scope compliance training, the first question we ask is: what do you want people to do differently after this? Not: what does the regulation require you to cover?

Those two questions produce completely different modules.

### Issue 3 — Microlearning

**Subject:** When 5 minutes beats a full-day course
**Preview text:** Not shorter training. Different training.

---

Hi [First Name],

We ran an informal comparison for a client last year. They had a 45-minute annual health and safety refresher that everyone completed in January and promptly forgot by March.

We rebuilt it as six 5-minute modules, delivered across six months. Each module covered one scenario from the original course. Same content. Different delivery.

Assessment scores at month six were 40% higher than the previous year. Manager-reported incidents of unsafe behaviour dropped.

This is not magic. It is how memory works.

The brain does not store information well when it receives it all at once. It stores it through repeated exposure over time — a pattern psychologists call spaced repetition. Microlearning is not just a trend. It is, when done correctly, a delivery method designed around how people actually retain information.

What it is not: a cheaper version of a proper course. A 5-minute module that tries to do everything a 45-minute course does is just a bad 45-minute course with most of the context removed.

The question to ask is: what does someone need to know in the moment they are doing the work? That is what you build the microlearning around.

For reinforcement, policy updates, safety reminders, and ongoing skill development — microlearning is hard to beat. For building foundational understanding of something complex, longer-form content still has a role.
